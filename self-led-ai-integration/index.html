Why We Do This

This Started Inside Our Own Engagements

Anna spent over a decade running operations inside fast-moving organizations before co-founding FINO. She knows what it looks like when capable people are buried under tools they did not choose.

When we started working with teams on Self-leadership, the same thing kept coming up after sessions. The inner work was shifting things. But on Monday morning, people were still drowning in AI tools nobody had time to learn, fears nobody had named, and the constant background hum of “we are falling behind.”

Some people were already deep in AI. Some were quietly resisting. Most were in the middle, too busy to try, unsure where to start.

So we built the offering. The Self-leadership practice we already do with teams, joined to operational and AI integration work built on years inside fast-moving organizations. One engagement, both sides at once.

What We Build

The Integration Most AI Rollouts Are Missing

A team that knows how to meet AI together.

Most AI training tries to override what people are feeling. Push the skeptic. Slow the enthusiast. Tell the middle group to try it. We do the opposite. We bring IFS-based practice into the room, name what every part of the team is bringing to this technology, and build a shared language for it. The resistance softens. The overwhelm gets workable. People stop performing okayness around AI and start using it for real.

AI in the actual work, not in a workshop.

We sit with your team in your real workflows. We see what is costing hours every week. We build the automations, write the prompts, set up a shared AI workspace for the team, and stay beside the people who will use it as they try it on real tasks. Not theory. Real implementation. By week three, your team is using tools they understand.

Team AI norms your people wrote together.

Most organizations adopt AI by accident. Someone uses one AI for one thing, someone else uses another for something else, and nobody ever names what the team agrees on. We facilitate the conversation. By the end of the engagement your team has written its own AI norms: what is okay, what is not, what stays human, what gets augmented. Not a policy handed down. A practice the team owns.

The Framework

Self-Leadership Applied to AI

In IFS, the qualities of Self-leadership are called the 8Cs. We map them onto how teams meet AI. Every engagement walks through these.

Curiosity and Calm

Meeting new tools with grounded openness.

Clarity and Compassion

Knowing what your team wants from AI.

Confidence and Courage

Trusting your own judgment when AI suggests something.

Creativity and Connectedness

AI in service of what is most human about your work.

The workshops and the implementation both move through these.

What This Looks Like in Practice

How an engagement unfolds.

Every team is different. Engagements run 60 to 90 days, custom-shaped to the team. The shape holds the arc. The details get built around your team.

Phase 1
We meet the team where it is.

A kickoff with the cohort. Everyone meets us. People ask the questions they have been carrying. We map who is where with AI without judgment. No technical work yet.

Phase 2
Inner work first.

Before we touch tools, we slow the pace down and bring IFS-based practice into the room. People name what they are bringing to AI: the fears, the curiosity, the hopes, the resistance. The cohort starts building shared language for the work ahead.

Phase 3
AI in the actual work.

We are in your workflows alongside the team. The most tedious tasks come off your plate first. People see real time savings. Practice continues alongside implementation, deepening as the work gets more visible.

Phase 4
The team owns the practice.

Your tools are built. Your team has its own shared language and its own AI norms, written together. We close the engagement with everything documented. Some teams continue with monthly partnership. Some take the practice and run it. Both are good outcomes.
